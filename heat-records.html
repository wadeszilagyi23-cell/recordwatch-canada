Why Heat Records Are Falling More Often

Weather still changes from day to day, but the background climate is warming. That shift makes record high temperatures—and especially record-warm nights—more likely than they used to be.

RecordWatch Canada Based on ECCC and IPCC assessments

A weather record is a comparison with the past. At each observing location, today’s maximum or minimum temperature is checked against measurements from the same calendar date in earlier years. When the climate itself becomes warmer, the entire range of possible temperatures shifts upward. Cool days still occur, but the odds increasingly favour new high-temperature records over new low-temperature records.

The central idea

Climate warming does not make every day hotter than the last. It raises the baseline on which ordinary weather variations occur, increasing the chance that a naturally hot weather pattern will reach or exceed an old record.

What does it mean to “break” a heat record?

RecordWatch Canada tracks two different kinds of warm-temperature records. They describe different parts of the daily temperature cycle and can occur together at the same location.

Record high maximum temperature

The day’s highest temperature exceeds the previous highest value observed for that calendar date at the station or climate location.

Record high minimum temperature

The day’s lowest temperature—often the overnight low—is warmer than the previous highest minimum for that calendar date.

A record can be broken by a large margin or by only a fraction of a degree. It can also be tied. The length and completeness of the observing period matter: breaking a record from a long, consistent climate history is more informative than breaking one from a short record.

A warming climate shifts the odds

Daily temperatures vary because of changing air masses, clouds, wind, soil moisture, snow cover and large-scale atmospheric patterns. Those influences produce the familiar ups and downs of weather. Climate change adds a long-term warming influence beneath those variations.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change has concluded that hot extremes have become more frequent and more intense across most land regions since the 1950s, while cold extremes have generally become less frequent and less severe. Human-caused climate change is the main driver of the observed global increase in hot extremes.

What the Canadian evidence shows

Canada has warmed more rapidly than the global average, although the amount of warming varies substantially by season and region. Northern Canada has experienced especially rapid warming.

These statistics do not mean that every location warms smoothly or that every year sets new records. Natural variability remains powerful. Some summers are cooler, and individual stations can show different short-term behaviour. The national pattern emerges by examining many locations over long periods.

Why record-warm nights matter

Heat records are often discussed in terms of afternoon highs, but unusually warm nights can be just as important. When temperatures remain high overnight, buildings and bodies have less opportunity to cool. Environment and Climate Change Canada reports a clearer national increase in overnight intensity during extreme heat events than in daytime intensity over its 1948–2023 analysis period.

Record high minimum temperatures may be encouraged by humid air, persistent cloud cover, warm water nearby, urban heat retention or a large-scale weather pattern that prevents nighttime cooling. Long-term climate warming raises the starting point on which those local influences operate.

Weather patterns still decide where records fall

Climate change changes the probabilities; it does not dictate the exact weather on a particular date. Individual heat records still depend on meteorology. Common contributors include:

  • persistent ridges of high atmospheric pressure;
  • warm air transported from another region;
  • dry soils that reduce evaporative cooling;
  • downslope winds that warm as air descends;
  • clear skies and strong summer sunshine; and
  • high humidity or cloud cover that limits overnight cooling.

A naturally occurring hot pattern can therefore still be the immediate cause of a record. In a warmer climate, however, that same kind of pattern begins from a higher baseline and has a better chance of reaching record-breaking territory.

Does one record prove climate change?

One record is weather

A single record at one station cannot, by itself, establish a long-term climate trend. Measurement history, station changes, local conditions and natural variability all need to be considered.

The pattern is climate

When hot records become more common across many stations and decades, while cold records become less common, that broader imbalance is consistent with a warming climate.

Event-attribution studies go a step further. They use observations and climate models to compare the chance and intensity of an event in today’s climate with a climate without the same human influence. Environment and Climate Change Canada’s rapid attribution system reported that human-caused climate change made almost all of the 37 major Canadian heat waves it analyzed from June through September 2024 hotter and more likely.

How to interpret RecordWatch Canada

RecordWatch Canada is a daily discovery and visualization tool. It identifies preliminary daily records reported through Environment and Climate Change Canada’s Long-Term Climate Extremes data and shows where they occurred.

The daily map is not, on its own, a climate-trend analysis. A cluster of heat records can be noteworthy, but understanding long-term change requires consistent records, quality-controlled datasets and statistical analysis across many years. Daily records are best viewed as individual pieces of a much larger climate picture.

Key takeaways

  • A warmer climate shifts the range of daily temperatures upward, increasing the odds of record highs.
  • Weather variability continues, so cold days and occasional cold records do not disappear.
  • Record-warm nights are especially important because they reduce overnight relief from heat.
  • One record is not proof of a trend, but the growing frequency and intensity of hot extremes across many regions is a well-established climate signal.
  • Daily RecordWatch results are preliminary and should be interpreted alongside long-term, quality-controlled climate information.
