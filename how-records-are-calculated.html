How Weather Records Are Calculated

From a thermometer or precipitation gauge to a century-long comparison: here is how Canadian daily observations become the records shown on RecordWatch Canada.

RecordWatch CanadaMethodology explainerLast reviewed: July 2026

RecordWatch Canada does not operate weather stations or create official climate records. It uses Environment and Climate Change Canada’s Long Term Climate Extremes database—usually abbreviated LTCE—which identifies daily temperature, precipitation and snowfall extremes for roughly 750 Canadian locations.

A daily record is a calendar-date comparison

A value observed for July 21 is compared with values from previous July 21 dates in that location’s climate record. It is not compared with every day of the year, and it is not the same as an all-time station record.

The process at a glance

1

Weather is observed

Temperature, rain, snow and other elements are measured at climate stations. Daily values may come from stations reporting once or twice per day, or may be derived from hourly observations.

2

Data enter the archive

Observations are transmitted to the National Climate Archive and initially screened. The amount of later review can vary, and published values remain subject to revision.

3

Stations are threaded

Nearby current and historical stations are joined into a long “virtual” climate station so that closures, relocations and station replacements do not leave only short fragments.

4

Each date is ranked

Values for the same month and day are ranked across the available years. July 21 competes with earlier July 21 values; February 29 is treated as its own date.

5

Records and ties are identified

A new value that exceeds the applicable extreme is a broken record. An equal value at the published precision is a tie.

6

RecordWatch presents the result

The site retrieves the latest LTCE fields, calculates display details such as margin and record age, and publishes the map, summaries and archive.

What is a climatological day?

A daily climate value does not always run from local midnight to local midnight. Meteorological services use a defined observation period called the climatological day so that daily maximum and minimum temperatures and accumulated precipitation are assigned consistently.

For many continuously operating Canadian climate stations, the temperature and precipitation day has ended at 0600 UTC on the following day since July 1, 1961. Observation practices differed in earlier periods and can also differ at stations that report once or twice per day. This means that a record labelled with a particular climate date may include observations made during part of the following calendar day.

Why this matters

The climate date is the date assigned under the observing rules. It should not always be interpreted as a strict midnight-to-midnight local calendar period.

The six record categories

LTCE publishes four temperature extremes and one extreme each for precipitation and snowfall. All are calculated separately for every day of the year.

High maximum temperature

The highest daily maximum temperature observed for that calendar date.

High minimum temperature

The warmest daily minimum temperature—often the warmest overnight low—for that date.

Low maximum temperature

The lowest daily maximum temperature, sometimes described as the coldest daytime high.

Low minimum temperature

The lowest daily minimum temperature observed for that date.

Greatest precipitation

The greatest water-equivalent total of all forms of precipitation, reported in millimetres.

Greatest snowfall

The greatest measured depth of newly fallen snow, reported in centimetres.

How the previous record and margin are calculated

The LTCE fields include the current record value and year, as well as the previous record value and year. When the current record belongs to the newest climate date, RecordWatch Canada displays both values and calculates the difference.

Previous record26.7 mm

The record margin is 17.7 mm. If both values had been 44.4 mm at the published precision, the new observation would be identified as a tied record rather than a broken one.

For low-temperature records, “more extreme” means lower rather than higher. RecordWatch may display a signed difference, while summary features such as Largest record margin use the absolute size of the difference.

Margins in different units are not directly comparable

A difference of 10 mm, 10 cm and 10 °C represents three different measurements. The Largest record margin card identifies the largest numerical change in its own source unit; it is not a scientific ranking of the severity of unlike weather elements.

Why nearby stations are joined together

A single observing site rarely remains unchanged for more than a century. Stations open and close, airports replace downtown sites, instruments move, and station names change. If records were calculated only from one station identifier, many locations would have short and fragmented histories.

The virtual climate station

ECCC begins with a representative active station for an urban location and follows its record backward. When that segment ends, another nearby station is selected using data quality, proximity, record gaps and regional expertise. The segments are joined into a long thread.

20 kmNormal search radius around an urban centre for candidate stations

In a small percentage of cases, ECCC may use a representative active station as far as approximately 35 km from the urban centre when no suitable active station is available within 20 km. The exact thread can also differ by weather element; temperature, precipitation and snowfall do not necessarily use the same component stations.

ECCC labels these source locations as an “Area” to show that the record represents a threaded urban-area history rather than one instrument at one fixed point. RecordWatch Canada shortens the visible community name by omitting the word “Area,” but the underlying LTCE record remains an area-based virtual climate station.

How tied records are handled

When a new observation is identical to an older record, ECCC keeps the oldest year as the official record year displayed for that date. Tied values are retained in the LTCE database among its ranked extremes. RecordWatch Canada separately identifies a current tied event so visitors can see that the old record was matched.

This convention prevents the official record year from changing every time the same value reoccurs, while preserving the existence of newer ties.

What does “period of record” mean?

The period shown in a popup, such as 1951–2026, indicates the beginning and current end of the relevant LTCE thread. It does not guarantee that every date in every year is complete. Some virtual stations contain gaps, and the available period can differ between maximum temperature, minimum temperature, precipitation and snowfall.

A longer period generally creates a more demanding record comparison because more historical years are available. However, record length alone does not measure data quality or meteorological importance.

Why a published record can later change

Late or corrected observations

A station report may arrive late, be corrected, or receive additional review after its first appearance.

Archive revisions

All archived climate data are subject to revision. Updates flow into LTCE during its regular refresh, which occurs twice daily.

Thread changes

New stations, closures and improved information can lead ECCC to revise which component stations form a virtual climate thread.

ECCC states that all incoming archive data are initially screened, but the amount of subsequent quality review varies and not every source receives the same level of review. RecordWatch therefore labels its daily output as preliminary.

Can these records be used to measure climate change?

LTCE is designed to identify and monitor daily extremes, not to calculate climate trends. Threading stations helps create long daily record histories, but it does not remove every non-climatic discontinuity caused by station moves, observing changes or instruments.

ECCC advises using its Adjusted and Homogenized Canadian Climate Data for formal climate-change and trend analysis. RecordWatch Canada’s daily records are valuable for discovery and historical context, but a single day—or even a cluster of daily records—is not by itself a complete trend analysis.

Key takeaways

  • Daily records compare the same calendar date across earlier years.
  • Canadian climate days do not always correspond exactly to local midnight-to-midnight periods.
  • ECCC joins nearby station histories into virtual climate stations to create longer records.
  • High records require a higher value; low temperature records require a lower value.
  • Precipitation is measured in millimetres of water equivalent, while snowfall is measured in centimetres of new-snow depth.
  • Ties are retained, but the oldest year remains the official record year.
  • Results are preliminary and may change after data or station-thread revisions.
