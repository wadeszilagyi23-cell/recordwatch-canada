How Weather Records Are Calculated

A daily weather record begins with an observation and ends with a comparison against the same date in the past. Here is how to interpret the temperature, precipitation and snowfall records shown on RecordWatch Canada.

RecordWatch CanadaMethodology explainerLast reviewed: July 2026

RecordWatch Canada presents daily record information published from Environment and Climate Change Canada observations. The site does not operate weather stations and does not declare official records. Its purpose is to organize published results into an accessible national map, summary and archive.

A daily record is a calendar-date comparison

A value assigned to July 21 is compared with values assigned to earlier July 21 dates for that location. It is not compared with every other day of the year, and it is not necessarily the location’s all-time highest or lowest value.

The process at a glance

1

Weather is measured

Instruments and observing programs measure temperature, precipitation, snowfall and other conditions at locations across Canada.

2

Observations are archived

Daily observations are collected and stored. Initial checks help identify obvious errors, while additional review or corrections may occur later.

3

The same date is compared

Each day’s value is compared with the available history for the same month and day at that location.

4

A record or tie is identified

A value beyond the previous extreme breaks the record. A value equal to it at the published precision ties the record.

5

The result is presented

RecordWatch Canada displays the location, new value, previous record, year, margin and available period of record.

✓

The record remains preliminary

Published climate values can be corrected or revised. RecordWatch results should therefore be treated as preliminary.

What is a climatological day?

A daily climate value is assigned according to established observing practices. The observation period does not always match a local midnight-to-midnight calendar day. Depending on the station and historical period, a daily maximum, minimum or precipitation total may include observations from part of the following calendar day.

Why this matters

The date shown is the official climate date assigned to the observation. It should not always be interpreted as a strict local calendar-day period.

The six record categories

RecordWatch Canada presents four temperature categories and one category each for precipitation and snowfall. Each category is evaluated separately.

High maximum temperature

The highest daily maximum temperature for that calendar date.

High minimum temperature

The warmest daily minimum temperature—often the warmest overnight low—for that date.

Low maximum temperature

The lowest daily maximum temperature, sometimes described as the coldest daytime high.

Low minimum temperature

The lowest daily minimum temperature observed for that date.

Precipitation

The greatest water-equivalent total of liquid and solid precipitation, reported in millimetres.

Snowfall

The greatest measured depth of newly fallen snow, reported in centimetres.

How the previous record and margin are calculated

The new observation is displayed beside the previous record for the same date. RecordWatch Canada then calculates the numerical difference between the two values.

Worked example: precipitation

New record44.4 mm
−
Previous record26.7 mm

The record margin is 17.7 mm. If both values were 44.4 mm at the published precision, the newer observation would be identified as a tie.

For low-temperature records, the new record is lower rather than higher. A signed difference may therefore appear as a negative value, while summary features can use the absolute size of the margin.

Different units should not be compared directly

A margin of 10 mm, 10 cm and 10 °C describes three different measurements. The Largest record margin card identifies the largest numerical change in its own unit; it is not a scientific ranking of the severity of unlike weather events.

Why a location’s record may include more than one observing site

Weather stations do not always remain in one place forever. They may close, move, change names or be replaced by another nearby station. To preserve a longer local history, a published climate record may sometimes combine compatible observations from more than one nearby site.

A local climate history

The published community record is intended to represent a useful long-term history for the local area rather than the lifetime of only one instrument.

Not always one fixed point

Some location names represent a broader local climate area. RecordWatch Canada shortens those names for readability, but the underlying record may draw from more than one compatible observing location over time.

This approach helps create a longer comparison period, but it is also one reason daily record information should be interpreted with care. The observing environment, instruments and procedures can change over a long history.

How tied records are handled

A tied record occurs when the new value equals the existing record at the precision being reported. The earlier year generally remains the historical record year, while the newer occurrence is still recognized as a tie.

This avoids replacing the original year each time the same value reoccurs and allows visitors to distinguish between a record that was exceeded and one that was matched.

What does “period of record” mean?

A period such as 1951–2026 indicates the approximate span of the published local record being used for comparison. It does not necessarily mean that every single day in every year is complete. Gaps can exist, and the available period can differ between temperature, precipitation and snowfall.

A longer period usually means the new value has been compared with more historical years. However, record length alone does not measure data quality or the meteorological importance of an event.

Why a published record can later change

Late observations

Some station reports can arrive after the first daily summary has been published.

Corrections and review

A value may be corrected after additional checks identify a reporting, instrument or transcription issue.

Historical revisions

Improvements to the published climate history can alter the value or year of a previous record.

For these reasons, RecordWatch Canada describes its daily results as preliminary. The site may update when revised information becomes available.

Can daily records be used to measure climate change?

Daily records provide valuable historical context, but they are not a complete climate-trend analysis. Long-term trend studies must account for changes in station location, instruments, observing practices, missing data and other non-climatic influences.

Climate researchers use specially prepared, quality-controlled datasets and statistical methods when assessing long-term change. A single daily record is weather. A persistent pattern across many locations, years and carefully analyzed datasets is climate.

Key takeaways

  • Daily records compare the same calendar date across earlier years.
  • A climate date does not always match a strict local midnight-to-midnight period.
  • High records require a higher value; low temperature records require a lower value.
  • Precipitation is measured as water equivalent in millimetres, while snowfall is measured as new-snow depth in centimetres.
  • Some long local histories may incorporate compatible observations from more than one nearby site.
  • A tie matches the old value; a broken record exceeds it in the applicable direction.
  • Daily results are preliminary and can be revised.
